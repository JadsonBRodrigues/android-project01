The History of the Android Mascot

You are probably aware that the Android operating system, developed and maintained by Google, is one of the most widely used platforms for mobile devices worldwide. However, did you know that its cheerful mascot has a name and a fascinating backstory? Follow this article to learn all about this adorable little robot.

The First Version

The first attempt to create a mascot was made in 2007 by a developer named Dan Morrill. He recalls opening Inkscape (a free and open-source image vectorisation software) and designing his own version of a robot. The goal was simply to give the system a persona for his team; there was no official request from the company to create a mascot.

This weird first version was even named in honour of its creator: they were called the Dandroids.

A new mascot is born

The idea of having a mascot began to mature, and the mission was assigned to a professional in the field. Russian illustrator Irina Blok, who was also a Google employee, was tasked with representing the small robot in a more appealing way.

Irina's main idea was to represent everything graphically with few lines and in a more flat design. The drawing also needed to quickly establish identification with the viewer. This led to the creation of the Bugdroid, the new Android mascot.

The main inspiration for the new Bugdroid's design came from the small figures used on restroom doors to indicate gender. Legend has it that the artist was working at her desk at the Google office when she glanced at the bathroom signs, and the idea hit her instantly: simple, clean, and direct.

Do you want to learn more?

Another curious aspect of Android is that each version was named after a dessert, following alphabetical order from version 1.5 to 9.0.

  • 1.5 - Cupcake
  • 1.6 - Donut
  • 3.0 - Eclair
  • 2.2 - Froyo
  • 2.3 - Gingerbread
  • 3.0 - Honeycomb
  • 4.0 - Ice Cream Sandwich
  • 4.1 - Jelly Bean
  • 4.4 - KitKat
  • 5.0 - Lolipop
  • 6.0 - Marshmallow
  • 7.0 - Nougat
  • 8.0 - Oreo
  • 9.0 - Pie

Unfortunately, Android Q never came to be, as Google decided to end the tradition of naming each version after a dessert. Instead, they switched to numerical naming, which led to Android 10.
